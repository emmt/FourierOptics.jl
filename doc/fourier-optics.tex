\section{Analytic Model}

The diffraction pattern by a circular (possibly with an obstruction), a
rectangular or an hexagonal perfect pupil has a closed (analytical) form. 
Accounting for the pixel integration amounts to convolving the analytical
PSF with the \emph{pixel shape}.  If the pixel grid is aligned with the
pupil sides, a closed form expression can be computed for a rectangular
pupil with rectangular pixels.

The complex amplitude diffracted by a square pupil of size $a \times a$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
  h(\V s) = \Paren*{\frac{\sin\Paren*{q}}{q}}^{\!\!2}
\end{equation}


\section{Numerical Simulation}

\subsection{Perfect lens}

Propagation of the complex amplitude between the pupil plane and the focal
plane of a (perfect) lens is done by:
\begin{align}
  \FT{a}(\V s) = \frac{1}{\lambda\,f} \, \iint p(\V r - \V c) \, a(\V r) \, \mathe^{
    \frac{-2\,\mathi\,\pi}{\lambda\,f}\,\Inner{\V r - \V c}{\V s - \V c}
  } \, \mathd^2\V r \, ,
  \label{eq:lens-transform}
\end{align}
where $\FT{a}(\V s)$ is the complex amplitude at the position $\V s$ of the
focal plane, $\FT{a}(\V r)$ is the complex amplitude at the position $\V r$
of the pupil plane, $p(\V r)$ is the complex amplitude transmission of the
pupil (relative to the center of the lens), $\V c$ is the position of the
center of the lens in the pupil plane, $\lambda$ is the wavelength and $f$
is the focal length. Equation~(\ref{eq:lens-transform}) involves a
continuous Fourier transform which we want to compute quickly by means of a
fast Fourier transform (FFT).

When the model is discretized, the complex amplitudes $a(\V r)$ and
$\FT{a}(\V s)$ are sampled at evenly spaced positions.  For instance:
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  a_{\V j} &\approx a(\V r_{\V j}) \,, \\
  p_{\V j} &\approx p(\V r_{\V j} - \V c) \,, \\
  \FT{a}_{\V k} &\approx \FT{a}(\V s_{\V k}) \,,
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
with $\V j \in \Omega$ and $\V k \in \Omega$ the 2D indices of the
samples in, respectively, the input and output plane and:
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  \V r_{\V j} &= (\V j - \V j_0) \times \Delta r \, , \\
  \V s_{\V k} &= (\V k - \V k_0) \times \Delta s \, ,
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where $\Delta r$ and $\Delta s$ are the sampling sizes (assumed to be the
same along the two dimensions) in the pupil plane and in the focal plane
while $\V j_0$ and $\V k_0$ are suitable offsets (not necessarily
integers).  The set $\Omega \subset \Integers^2$ of sampling indices has
size $N_1 \times N_2$.  Using the same conventions as for the 2D discrete
Fourier transform, $\Omega$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \Omega = \IntegerRange{0,N_1-1}\times\IntegerRange{0,N_2-1} \, .
\end{equation}
As a result of choosing identical sampling sizes along all dimensions, we
have to take the same number of samples in every dimension (hence
$N_1=N_2=N$). \oops{[OK but explain why.]}

For discretized complex amplitudes, the continuous Fourier transform is
approximated by a discrete Fourier transform (DFT) defined by:
\begin{equation}
  \DFT{B}_{\V k}
  \bydef \sum_{\V j \in \Omega}
  B_{\V j} \, \mathe^{-2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j}{\V k}_{\Omega}} \, ,
  \label{eq:DFT}  
\end{equation}
where the inner product on $\Omega$ is defined by:
\begin{equation}
  \Inner{\V j}{\V k}_{\Omega} = \sum\nolimits_d \frac{j_d\,k_d}{N_d} \, ,
\end{equation}
where the sum is taken over the number of dimensions ($2$ for us).

Approximating the integral in Eq.~(\ref{eq:lens-transform}) by a Riemann
sum yields:
\begin{equation}
  \FT{a}_{\V k} \approx \frac{\Delta r^2}{\lambda\,f} \,
  \sum_{\V j \in \Omega} p_{\V j} \, a_{\V j} \,
  \mathe^{\frac{-2\,\mathi\,\pi}{\lambda\,f}\,\Inner{\V r_{\V j}}{\V s_{\V k}}} \, .
  \label{eq:Riemann-approx}
\end{equation}
In order to make this looks like a DFT, the following relation must hold:
\begin{displaymath}
  \Inner{\V j - \V j_0}{\V k - \V k_0}_{\Omega}
  = \frac{\Inner{\V r_{\V j}}{\V s_{\V k}}}{\lambda\,f} \, ,
  \label{eq:dimensioning}
\end{displaymath}
which implies that:
\begin{equation}
  N\,\Delta r\,\Delta s = \lambda\,f \, .
\end{equation}
This relation relates the sampling sizes in the two planes.  Note that the
quantities $\Delta r$, $\Delta s$, $\lambda$ and $f$ are all lengths.
Assuming the relation in Eq.~(\ref{eq:dimensioning}) holds, the complex
amplitude in the focal plane is approximately given by:
\begin{align}
  \FT{a}_{\V k}
  &\approx \frac{\Delta r^2}{\lambda\,f} \, \sum_{\V j \in \Omega}
  p_{\V j} \, a_{\V j} \,
  \mathe^{-2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j - \V j_0}{\V k - \V k_0}_{\Omega}} \,
  \notag\\
  &= \frac{\Delta r^2}{\lambda\,f} \,
  \mathe^{+2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j_0}{\V k - \V k_0}_{\Omega}} \,
  \sum_{\V j \in \Omega}
  \mathe^{+2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j}{\V k_0}_{\Omega}} \,
  p_{\V j} \, a_{\V j} \,
  \mathe^{-2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j}{\V k}_{\Omega}} \, ,  
\end{align}
where the sum in the last right hand side is the DFT of:
\begin{displaymath}
  \mathe^{+2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j}{\V k_0}_{\Omega}} \, p_{\V j} \,
  a_{\V j} \, .
\end{displaymath}
If the offsets $\V j_0$ and $\V k_0$ have integer coordinates, another way
to write this is:
\begin{equation}
  \FT{a}_{\V k + \V k_0} \approx \frac{\Delta r^2}{\lambda\,f} \,
  \sum_{\V j \in \Omega} p_{\V j + \V j_0} \, a_{\V j + \V j_0} \,
  \mathe^{-2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j}{\V k}_{\Omega}} \, ,
\end{equation}
where circular shift is used for translating the sampled arrays.  If the
\emph{origin of coordinates} is defined following the usual convention,
then the offsets $\V j_0$ and $\V k_0$ have integer coordinates and the
equation becomes:
\begin{align}
  \FT{a}_{\V k} &\approx \frac{\Delta r^2}{\lambda\,f} \, 
  \mathtt{fftshift}\Paren*{
    \sum_{\V j \in \Omega}
    \mathtt{ifftshift}\Paren*{\Diag(\V p) ⋅ \V a}_{\V j} \,
  \mathe^{-2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j}{\V k}_{\Omega}}
  }_{\V k} \\
  &= \frac{\Delta r^2}{\lambda\,f} \,
    \mathtt{fftshift}\Paren*{
      \mathtt{fft}\Paren*{
        \mathtt{ifftshift}\Paren*{\Diag(\V p) ⋅\V a}
      }
    }_{\V k} \, ,
\end{align}
where $\V a$ denotes the array of sampled values of the complex amplitude
in the pupil plane.

\subsection{Simulating a tip-tilt}

The tip-tilt $\V\theta = \delta\V s/f$ (with $\delta\V s$ the displacement
of the image in the focal plane) yields a phase shift $\phi(\V r)$ such that:
\begin{displaymath}
  \phi(\V r) - \frac{2\,\pi}{\lambda\,f}\,\Inner{\V r}{\V s}
  = -\frac{2\,\pi}{\lambda\,f}\,\Inner{\V r}{\V s - \delta\V s} \, .
\end{displaymath}
Hence $\phi(\V r)$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \phi(\V r) = \frac{2\,\pi}{\lambda\,f}\,\Inner{\V r}{\delta\V s}
  = \Inner{\V u}{\V r} \, ,
\end{equation}
with:
\begin{equation}
  \V u = \frac{2\,\pi\,\delta\V s}{\lambda\,f} = \frac{2\,\pi\,\V\theta}{\lambda}\,.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Potential issues}

\paragraph{Pupil plane aliasing.}  To avoid aliasing when computing
intensities, the size $D$ of the pupil should not be larger than $N\,\Delta
r/2$.

\paragraph{Vignetting.}  To avoid vignetting, the input region must encompass the
the pupil.

\paragraph{Focal plane periodisation.}  To avoid this issue, the considered
output field must not be too far from the direction of propagation
approximately defined by the tip-tilt of the wavefront just after the
pupil.


\subsection{Lazy Algebra Implementation}

Using linear algebra notation, the effect of a lens can be represented by a
linear operator $\M L$.  Equation~(\ref{eq:lens-transform}) becomes:
\begin{equation}
  \DFT{\V a} = \M L⋅\V a \, ,
\end{equation}
with:
\begin{equation}
  \M L = \Diag\Paren[big]{\V q^\Tag{out}}⋅\M F⋅\Diag\Paren[big]{\V q^\Tag{inp}} \, ,
\end{equation}
where $\M F$ is the FFT operator (of appropriate size) and:
\begin{align}
  q^\Tag{inp}_{\V j} &= \frac{\Delta r^2}{\lambda\,f} \, p_{\V j} \,
  \mathe^{+2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j}{\V k_0}_{\Omega}} \, , \\
  q^\Tag{out}_{\V k} &= 
  \mathe^{+2\,\mathi\,\pi \, \Inner{\V j_0}{\V k - \V k_0}_{\Omega}} \, .
\end{align}
The parameters to define $\M L$ are: $\V p$ the sampled pupil transmission,
$N$ the size of the FFT, $\lambda$ the wavelength, $f$ the focal length,
$\Delta r$ and $\Delta s$ the sampling steps in the pupil and focal plane,
the offsets $\V j_0$ and $\V k_0$ of the origin in these two planes.  These parameters are related by the relation in Eq.~(\ref{eq:dimensioning}).

Example, the wavefront sensor of Themis AO system has pixel of size $\Delta s = 7\micron$, the focal length of the microlenses is $f = 14.57\,\mathrm{mm}$ and their diameter is $D = 300\,\micron$.  At $\lambda = 500\,\mathrm{nm}$,  Eq.~(\ref{eq:dimensioning}) yields:
\begin{displaymath}
    \Delta r = \frac{\lambda\,f}{N\,\Delta s} \approx \frac{1.04\,\mathrm{mm}}{N} \, .
\end{displaymath}
The number of samples across the pupil is given by:
\begin{displaymath}
    \frac{D}{\Delta r} = \frac{N\,D\,\Delta s}{\lambda\,f} \approx 0.288\,N \, .
\end{displaymath}
which is smaller than $N/2$ and thus there is no aliasing without the needs
to subsample the output pixels.  We must choose $N ≥ 14$ to have at least 4
pixels per pupil.  Since the output image is about $36\times36$\,pixels,
taking $N=64$ is sufficient to satisfy all constraints.

A complex amplitude means nothing without units and necessary parameters to figure out the physical position of a given sample.

\section{Cardinal Sine}

The transmission of a (mono-dimensional) rectangular pupil $p(r)$ of width
$a$ is proportional to $g_{a}(r)$ the normalized and centered rectangular
gate function of width $a$ defined by:
\begin{equation}
   g_{a}(r) = \frac{1}{a} \times \begin{cases}
   1   & \text{if $\Abs{r} < a/2$;}\\
   1/2 & \text{if $\Abs{r} = a/2$;}\\
    0  & \text{if $\Abs{r} > a/2$;}\\
   \end{cases}
   \label{eq:gate-function}
\end{equation}
and whose Fourier transform is:
\begin{align}
  \FT{g}_a(u)
  &= \int g_a(r)\,\mathe^{-\mathi\,2\,\pi\,r\,u}\,\mathd r
  = \frac{1}{a} \, \int_{-a/2}^{a/2} \mathe^{-2\,\pi\,\mathi\,r\,u}\,\mathd r
  \notag\\
  &= \frac{\sin\Paren{\pi\,a\,u}}{\pi\,a\,u} \\
  &= \sinc\Paren{a\,u}
\end{align}
where $\sinc$ is the \emph{cardinal sine function} defined by:
\begin{equation}
   \sinc(x) \bydef \begin{cases}
   1   & \text{if $x = 0$;}\\[1ex]
   \displaystyle \frac{\sin\Paren{\pi\,x}}{\pi\,x}
   & \text{if $x \not= 0$.}
   \end{cases}
   \label{eq:sinc-function}
\end{equation}

The PSF $h_\Tag{foc}(s)$ is proportional to a squared cardinal sine:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:psf-foc}
  h_\Tag{foc}(s) \propto \sinc^2\Paren*{\rho_\Tag{foc}\,s}
  \quad\text{with}\quad
  \rho_\Tag{foc} = \frac{a}{\lambda\,f} \, ,
\end{equation}
assuming $s$ is the position in the focal plane, $f$ is the focal length
and $\lambda$ is the wavelength.  Alternately, considering $\theta = s/f$
the angular direction of the light, the angular PSF writes:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:psf-ang}
  h_\Tag{ang}(\theta) \propto \sinc^2\Paren*{\rho_\Tag{ang}\,\theta}
  \quad\text{with}\quad
  \rho_\Tag{ang} = a/\lambda \, .
\end{equation}
As a general case, we consider that the PSF writes:
\begin{equation}
  h(x) \propto \sinc^2\Paren*{\rho\,x} \, ,
  \label{eq:psf-general}
\end{equation}
for some positional coordinate $x$ and with $\rho \propto a$ given by
Eq.~(\ref{eq:psf-foc}) or Eq.~(\ref{eq:psf-ang}).

To compute the normalization factor or to integrate the PSF in a pixel, a
primitive $G(x)$ of the squared cardinal sine is needed.  This primitive
has a closed form:
\begin{equation}
   G(x) \bydef \int_0^x \sinc^2(t) \, \mathd t
   = \frac{\sinint\Paren*{2\,\pi\,x}}{\pi} - x\,\sinc^2(x)\, .
\end{equation}
where $\sinint$ is the so called \emph{sine integral function} defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \sinint(x) \bydef \int_0^x \frac{\sin t}{t} \, \mathd t \,,
  \label{eq:sinint}
\end{equation}
which is defined for all $x \in \Reals$.  The limits of the sine integral
function are:
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{x\rightarrow\pm\infty}\sinint(x) = \pi/2 \, .
\end{equation}
The squared cardinal sine function is therefore normalized:
\begin{equation}
  \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \sinc^2(t)\,\mathd t = 1 \,.
\end{equation}

To have a normalized PSF, we must write the PSF as:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{
    h(x) = \rho\,\sinc^2\Paren{\rho\,x} \, .
  }
  \label{eq:slit-psf}
\end{equation}
A primitive of this PSF is:
\begin{align}
   H(x) &\bydef \int_0^x h(t) \, \mathd t \\
   &= \frac{\sinint\Paren*{2\,\pi\,\rho\,x}}{\pi} - \rho\,x\,\sinc^2(\rho\,x)\, .
\end{align}
